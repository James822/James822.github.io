Reduction Inference
2025 Feb 24, James Spratt

For around half a year now I've been deeply fascinated with systems of logic, many sorted logic (first order, second order, etc.), rewrite systems, proof theory, and the like.

Although my knowledge on the subject is certainly quite limited, I've come across some very interesting results when trying to define my own logic system.

Around last summer I attempt to create a rewrite system of my own that would accept first-order sentences, a set of axioms, and a then a list of transformations to act as an automated proof-checker. I never did quite finish this system, but even just working on the logical preliminaries expanded my knowledge by a significant amount, leading me to a key discovery that I have called "reduction inference".


Modus Ponens

One of the most interesting results that I came across when trying to develop the theory behind a proof system (a formal method to derive theorems from a set of axioms, using the rules of logic), is that all inference or reasoning is actually a reduction of information.

What I mean to say is that to infer or to deduce information is not, strictly speaking, creating any new information that did not exist prior. Rrather, it is a focus on a paticular subset of information that may have been previously readily apparent.

Consider the exampe of modus ponens, a familiar and ancient form logical inference that arises in classical logic and also mathematical logic. We can state it like such in propositional form:


P ∧ ( P ⟹ Q ) , therefore: Q

Let us focus on the P ∧ ( P ⟹ Q ) part and ignore the "therefore" (inference) part. Using the basic rules of logic, we can rewrite this formula as such:


P ∧ ( P ⟹ Q )
⟺ P ∧ ( ¬ P ∨ Q )
⟺ ( P ∧ ¬ P ) ∨ ( P ∧ Q )
⟺ P ∧ Q

And so we proved that the following logical equivalence is true:


[ P ∧ ( P ⟹ Q ) ] ⟺ [ P ∧ Q ]

So we can restate modus ponens in the following logically equivalent fashion:


P ∧ Q , therefore: Q

But this is clearly a loss of information! We went from assuming the truth of two propositions (namely P and Q), to only inferring that one such proposition Q is true. So we can readily conclude that all inference is essentially (logically) a reduction of information, rather than revealing anything new.

An insightful conclusion that we can derive from this experiment is that all true information that exists (specifically true statements that are decidable in a system), already exists within the system. Given a Formal System, such as a Hilbert-style system), we can conclude that deriving theorems from that system is not a revelation of new of information, but rather a focused subset of what the axioms entail.

This also gives arise to a notion of having the minimum information to prove something. We may conclude that for example Q is provable from P, but it may not be the case that everything in P is strictly neccessary to prove Q. In this case, there may exist a subset R of Q such that R is completely neccessary for Q. We might be interested in the smallest subset of axioms or theorems that is neccessary to prove a theroem


Focus

An important practical lesson that I've derived from this little experiment is that all of our reasoning is essentially a focused subset of what is relevant to us. At any given time we can only hold so much in our minds, but even less, we are only interested in a small subset of things for specific purposes. The example of modus ponens illustrates that we don't really care to emphasize what we already knew was true (P), but that we care about something that we weren't aware of being true (Q).

It was steve jobs that famously said that "focusing is about saying no", rather than saying "yes". There are an ifinite amount of considerations that can be had at any time, but such a small subset is only of concern to us at a given time for a specific problem, and therefore a signifcant portion of proving theorems and reasoning lies in the ability to focus on what is relevant, and ignore all else.
